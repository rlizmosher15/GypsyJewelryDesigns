How I make my own Sea Glass

Learn my tips and techniques

If you've happened upon this page you probably already know what sea glass is, but for those of you who might not, here's a clear definition. (get it.. clear!)

Sea glass is broken glass that has been discarded into a body of salt water, and tumbled by the waves and sand. The continuous tumbling wears down and rounds the edges of the glass, and gives it a smooth, frosted appearance.  

This process takes years to occur naturally, but I do it in just a few days!


Now, you will need some supplies to do this project! These include:
A Rock Tumbler
Course, Medium, and Fine Grit
Polish(optional depending on desired luster)
Saftely glasses
A Face Mask or covering(this should be an easy one)
... and of course, some broken glass!

Awesome! If you have all of these, you're ready to get started. 

Step 1: Make sure your glass is ready to tumble

Cut or smash your glass to the aproximate size or shape that you want. Be sure to wear your safety glasses and face mask!

Step 2: Coarse Grit

Once you're satisfied with the size and shape, put your raw glass in the tumbler with coarse grit. This will begin the breakdown process and take away the most material, rounding out the sharp edges. The glass should be in your tumbler for 3 days to a week, depending on the results you're looking for. 

Step 3: Medium Grit

Take the glass from your tumbler and wash it off in a bucket. Don't wash the sludge down your sink or it could clog up your drains! Make sure you get all the course grit and glass dust off so that the medium grit won't be contaminated with your rough grit. Also wash your tumbling barrel with the same thing in mind. Once your glass and barrel are completely dry, put your medium grit and glass back in the barrel and tumble away! Again, this should take a few days to a week.

Step 4: Fine Grit

Repeat the steps above for cleaning your glass and barrel, and tumble the glass in your fine grit.

Step 5: Polish

If you're looking for a super clear and bright appearance, you can repeat the steps above and use a polish in the barrel with your glass. If you want a frosted look, end your process with the fine grit.
